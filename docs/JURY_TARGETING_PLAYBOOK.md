# Jury Targeting Playbook — AI Cup 2026 Pitch

_Last updated: 2026-04-11_

## Use note
This playbook is for **audience adaptation**, not manipulation. It is built from **public professional information** plus clearly labeled role-based inference. It does **not** profile private emotions, vulnerabilities, or non-public beliefs.

Use it to answer one question:

> For each juror, what do they most likely care about, what will make them trust us, and what version of our true story will land best?

---

# Our master positioning
No matter who asks the question, keep the same core truth:

> We did not just chase a leaderboard score. We built a research-driven, evidence-based, deployment-aware system for bird classification from radar data that is meant to support nature-inclusive wind-energy decisions.

## Universal signals that help with all five jurors
- We understand the **real problem**: wind energy and biodiversity must be managed together.
- We used a disciplined **hypothesis -> experiment -> honest validation -> keep/reject** workflow.
- We learned that **temporal/month shift** is a core challenge, not a footnote.
- We treated the task as a **ranking problem** and aligned our modeling accordingly.
- We built a **system**, not a single black-box model.
- We respect **uncertainty**, **trade-offs**, and **human oversight**.
- Our team showed execution discipline, including winning the award for **most submissions** — framed as structured experimentation, not brute force.

# 1) Marelle van Beerschoten

## Verified public-professional profile
- Founding leader of **Digital Shapers**; public titles vary across sources (Founder / CEO / Owner-Director / Managing Director).
- Public themes: **digital transformation, AI adoption, data-driven work, leadership, change, future of work**.
- Public positioning from Digital Shapers emphasizes the **human side of transformation** and that people and technology should bring out the best in each other.
- Public bio notes: cum laude graduate of the **University of Amsterdam** and **University College Maastricht**; startup experience; Applified -> Digital Shapers evolution.

## Biggest likely interest
**Can this technology actually be adopted and used by real people in real organizations?**

## Professional attention profile (inference)
Marelle likely rewards:
- clarity over complexity
- practical value over novelty-for-novelty’s sake
- adoption logic over pure modeling detail
- trust, usability, explainability, and workflow fit
- signs of team maturity: what you tested, rejected, and learned

## What to emphasize
- We built for **adoption**, not only for accuracy.
- This is a **decision-support system**, not AI theatre.
- Robustness under changing conditions matters because organizations need systems they can trust.
- Human oversight is a feature, not a weakness.
- We understand operational trade-offs and workflow integration.

## What will likely resonate most
- "We designed the system to fit a real monitoring workflow."
- "We optimized for what generalizes, not just what looks good once."
- "This is AI designed to be used by people, not admired on a slide."

## What could lose her
- dense model talk with no operational story
- no mention of trust, oversight, or stakeholders
- hype without evidence
- acting like deployment is trivial
- making the project sound like an academic exercise only

## Best phrases for her
1. "We built a system that can fit into a real operational workflow."
2. "Our hardest problem wasn’t only accuracy; it was making the solution robust enough to be usable."
3. "We treated AI as an adoption problem as well as a technical problem."
4. "We kept what generalized and rejected what only looked good in isolated experiments."
5. "The goal is practical: responsible, explainable support for better wind-energy decisions."

## Best answer style
**Answer -> evidence -> limitation -> deployment consequence**

Example:
> We designed the system to support operational decisions, not replace them. We validated across different periods to reduce overfitting to one condition. The limitation is that uncertainty remains, which is why human oversight stays in the loop. That is also what makes the system realistic to deploy.

## Likely question themes
- How would this be adopted by an operator or organization?
- Why is this better than a simpler manual approach?
- How do you build trust in the output?
- What trade-offs remain?

## Best one-line sell
> We built a technically serious system, but we designed it to be used by people in the real world.

## Biggest likely interest

## Professional attention profile (inference)
Joep likely rewards:
- operational usefulness
- ecological validity
- mitigation relevance
- robustness across seasons and field conditions
- deployment realism, especially where manual monitoring is hard
- evidence that the system can support multiple stakeholders: operators, ecologists, regulators

- Our validation respected **seasonal / month shift**, because that is closer to the real deployment challenge.
- The output should support **curtailment / targeted mitigation / monitoring prioritization**, not blindly automate decisions.
- Offshore and complex conditions make scalable monitoring more valuable.

## What will likely resonate most
- "This turns sensor data into actionable monitoring insight."
- "We focused on ecological signal that can support decisions, not only score gains."
- "The value is whether operators and ecologists can trust it in changing conditions."

## What could lose him
- leaderboard-only framing
- no discussion of false positives / false negatives
- no mitigation story
- no uncertainty handling
- no deployment pathway
- treating ecology as secondary

## Best phrases for him
1. "We treated this as a monitoring-and-mitigation problem, not just a classification problem."
2. "Our system is built for wind-energy operations where manual observation is difficult, especially offshore."
3. "We focused on ecological signal that can actually inform decisions."

## Likely question themes

- How robust is it across season/weather/site changes?
- How would it support mitigation or curtailment?
- What do the errors mean operationally?

## Best one-line sell
> We built a wind-energy ecology system that turns sensor data into operationally useful monitoring and mitigation insight.

# 4) Geert-Jan Houben

## Verified public-professional profile
- **Pro Vice Rector Magnificus AI, Data and Digitalisation** at **TU Delft**.
- **Full Professor of Web Information Systems**.
- Public responsibilities span AI/data/digitalisation across education, research, valorisation, and support; involved in TU Delft AI Labs.
- Public themes around him include **open science, data sharing, data-centric AI, human-in-the-loop design, meaningful human control, and systems that work in context rather than only in theory**.

## Biggest likely interest
**Is this a rigorous, reproducible, context-aware AI system that really generalizes?**

## Professional attention profile (inference)
Geert-Jan likely rewards:
- rigor over hype
- systems thinking
- data + model + validation + deployment coherence
- honest handling of shift and failure modes
- human accountability in AI-supported decisions
- cross-disciplinary usefulness

## What to emphasize
- We aligned modeling with the fact that the competition metric is **macro-mAP**, a ranking metric.
- We treated **temporal shift** as a first-class failure mode and validated accordingly.
- We built a **reproducible decision-support pipeline**, not a one-off tuned model.
- We can explain which techniques failed and why we rejected them.
- We are explicit about uncertainty and the continued role of human oversight.

## What will likely resonate most
- "We optimized for generalization, not just benchmark performance."
- "This is an AI system designed in context: data, features, validation, and deployment all matter."
- "We used honest validation to reduce optimistic conclusions."

## What could lose him
- leaderboard talk without methodological justification
- overclaiming certainty
- black-box framing
- no explanation of temporal robustness
- no reproducibility story
- no honest account of failure modes and rejected methods

## Best phrases for him
1. "We optimized for generalization, not just benchmark performance."
2. "This is an AI system designed in context: radar trajectories, environmental signals, and operational validation work together."
3. "We treated temporal shift as a first-class failure mode, because real deployment does not look like one static split."
4. "Our approach is intentionally human-in-the-loop where it matters."
5. "We can explain what the system uses, why it works, and where caution is still needed."
6. "We designed this as a reproducible decision-support pipeline, not a one-off model."

## Best answer style
**System-level point -> evidence -> trade-off -> limit -> deployment implication**

Example:
> We designed the system for robustness across months because that is the closest proxy to real deployment shift in this dataset. That changed our feature selection and model choices. The trade-off is a more careful, less flashy pipeline, but it gives us better stability and a clearer case for deployment. It still does not eliminate ecological uncertainty, which is why we keep it as decision support rather than autonomous control.

## Likely question themes
- Why should we believe it generalizes?
- What did you do to prevent month-specific overfitting?
- Which parts are domain-designed vs learned?
- What are the main failure modes?
- How reproducible is the workflow?

## Best one-line sell
> We built a rigorous, context-aware, reproducible AI system for a real domain and validated it honestly against shift and deployment constraints.

# 5) Bart Kranstauber

## Verified public-professional profile
- Computational ecologist at **IBED, University of Amsterdam**.
- Publicly described as focusing on **animal movement**, radar-based study of airspace movement, predictive models for bird migration, and nature-inclusive wind energy.
- Public work around his name spans **radar ornithology, movement ecology, migration forecasting, wind effects on migration, aeroconservation, and mitigation-relevant ecological modeling**.

## Biggest likely interest
**Does the system make ecological sense and help conservation / mitigation decisions without overstating what radar can tell us?**

## Professional attention profile (inference)
Bart likely rewards:
- movement-aware modeling
- ecological validity
- seasonality / migration-aware reasoning
- uncertainty-aware claims
- outputs that support mitigation and management, not just benchmark ranking
- validation that reflects ecological shift

## What to emphasize
- We treated radar as an **ecological sensor**, not a species oracle.
- We combined **movement, radar, and environmental context** because ecological signal is distributed across them.
- We respected **seasonality / migration timing** in validation.
- The model output is **probabilistic decision support** for monitoring and curtailment, not overconfident automation.
- Our goal is better-targeted mitigation and conservation-relevant monitoring.

## What will likely resonate most
- "Movement-aware modeling rather than generic tabular ML."
- "Cross-month validation as a proxy for ecological robustness."
- "Respect for uncertainty and radar limitations."
- "Support for mitigation hierarchy and management decisions."

## What could lose him
- overclaiming species certainty from radar
- generic ML benchmark framing with no ecology logic
- ignoring seasonality / phenology / wind influence
- no real mitigation story
- weak validation under temporal shift
- acting like the model should directly automate intervention without human judgment

## Best phrases for him
1. "We treat radar as an ecological sensor, not a species oracle."
2. "Migration is seasonal, so our validation had to respect temporal shift."
3. "Our goal is better-targeted mitigation, not just a better leaderboard score."
4. "The model gives probabilistic support for monitoring and curtailment, with human judgment in the loop."
5. "We combined movement, radar, and context because the ecological signal lives across all three."

## Best answer style
**Ecological intuition -> feature representation -> validation evidence -> limitation -> operational implication**

Example:
> We’re not claiming radar gives exact species identity. We’re using movement, radar, and environmental context to infer probabilistic ecological patterns. That improves robustness, especially across seasonal shift, and makes the output more useful for mitigation and conservation decisions.

## Likely question themes
- How biologically meaningful are your features?
- How do you know this generalizes across seasons/weather?
- What would this change operationally for mitigation?
- How do you handle uncertain or ambiguous radar nights?
- What are the ecological limits?

## Best one-line sell
> This is an ecologically informed modeling pipeline that respects movement dynamics and uncertainty while making mitigation support more actionable.

# How to steer the same pitch to all five at once

## Opening 30 seconds
Pitch to **Marelle + Joep + Andy**:
- practical problem
- deployable system
- real value for nature-inclusive wind energy

## Middle technical minute
Pitch to **Geert-Jan + Bart**:
- why month shift mattered
- why ranking-aware modeling mattered
- why movement/context features mattered
- what you rejected and why

## Deployment + impact minute
Pitch back to **Joep + Andy + Marelle**:
- operator workflow
- mitigation use
- adoption path
- human oversight

## Closing 20 seconds
Aim for **all five**:
> We believe the strength of our work is not only that it performs well, but that it is rigorous, ecologically grounded, and realistically deployable.
