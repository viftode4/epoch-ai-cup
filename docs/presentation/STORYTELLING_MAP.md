# AI Cup Congress Pitch — Storytelling Map

_Last updated: 2026-04-11_

## Core rule
Every slide must answer 3 questions:
1. **What are we trying to make the audience feel or understand right now?**
2. **Why does this need to be shown visually instead of only said?**
3. **How does this move us toward the final message?**

## Final message we are building toward
> We are not just a model team; we are a research-driven team that built a rigorous, ecologically grounded, and realistically deployable AI system for biodiversity-aware wind-energy decisions.

## Locked slide order
1. Opening / identity
2. Problem framing
3. System snapshot
4. Workflow as credibility engine
5. Key insights
6. Proof / why trust it
7. Deployment / closing

---

## Slide 1 — Opening / identity
### Purpose
Make the room care immediately and frame the team correctly.

### What the audience should think
- this is a real problem
- this team has something serious to say
- this is bigger than a competition model

### What to show
- big statement: bird-safe wind energy needs trustworthy AI
- visual intersection of airspace / radar / wind / biodiversity
- minimal labels only

### Why this visual works
The opening should show **overlap** and **tension**, not details.
The visual should say:
- same airspace
- conflicting pressures
- structured system thinking

### What not to show
- metrics
- detailed architecture
- too many model names

---

## Slide 2 — Problem framing
### Purpose
Explain why the task matters in the real world.

### What the audience should think
- this is a real operational + ecological problem
- the challenge is multi-sided
- the system needs to satisfy more than accuracy

### What to show
Three-part frame:
- ecology
- operations
- monitoring challenge

### Why this visual works
A 3-part layout shows that the problem has multiple stakeholders.
That helps the whole room see themselves in the pitch.

### Visual representation
Use 3 clean cards or zones rather than a paragraph.
Include one note that this is **not just an accuracy problem**.

---

## Slide 3 — System snapshot
### Purpose
Give the room a concrete object early.

### What the audience should think
- okay, now I understand what they actually built
- this is technically coherent
- this system is ecology-aware and decision-support oriented

### What to show
A 4-stage system flow:
- radar trajectories + context
- behavior/context feature stack
- ranking-aware models
- probabilistic decision support

### Critical addition
Show the **seasonal/month shift constraint** as a side annotation or highlighted box.
Also explicitly state:
- radar is an ecological sensor, not a species oracle
- month shift reflects migration behavior, not just dataset drift

### Visual representation
Use one clean central figure + one highlighted constraint note.

---

## Slide 4 — Workflow as credibility engine
### Purpose
Establish that your workflow is why the system deserves trust.

### What the audience should think
- these people worked seriously
- they filtered noise into signal
- the result comes from disciplined reasoning, not luck

### What to show
A research loop:
- hypothesis
- experiment
- validation
- keep/reject
- robust system

### Why this must be visual
Your workflow is one of your strongest differentiators, but it should now explain the system rather than replace it.

### What to emphasize
- honest validation under shift
- rejected brittle ideas
- learning speed and judgment
- most submissions award only as a small throughput badge

### Visual representation
Use notebook / systems-diagram energy.
It should feel like a research filter or engine.

---

## Slide 5 — Key insights
### Purpose
Show that the project produced understanding, not only a result.

### What the audience should think
- they learned something important
- they changed strategy intelligently
- the workflow produced real insight

### What to show
Three major insights only:
1. ranking mattered
2. shift mattered
3. ecology/context had to stay inside the model logic

### Why this matters
This is the slide that upgrades you from “model builders” to “serious thinkers.”

### Visual representation
Three large insight cards with one notebook-style annotation.
This should feel like distilled research knowledge.

---

## Slide 6 — Proof / why trust it
### Purpose
Build credibility with both jury and room.

### What the audience should think
- they have evidence
- they explored broadly
- they are honest about evaluation
- they are worth taking seriously after this talk

### What to show
A proof structure with:
- **1 primary proof anchor**: AI Cup 2026 finalist status / top-finalist outcome
- **2 supporting evidence blocks**: robust validation under shift, breadth of experiments / rejected brittle methods
- **1 optional small chip**: most submissions award

### Why this visual works
A proof grid is faster and more memorable than a dense benchmark table.
It sells both the system and the team.

### Visual representation
Use structured evidence cards with technical / notebook detailing.
This should feel like a lab result sheet, not a brag slide.

---

## Slide 7 — Deployment / closing
### Purpose
Land the talk in the real world and make people remember you.

### What the audience should think
- this can actually be used
- the team understands deployment
- this matters beyond the competition

### What to show
An operational flow:
- radar monitoring
- AI ranking + uncertainty
- operator / ecologist review
- targeted mitigation action

### Closing role
End with a line that binds together:
- rigor
- ecology
- deployability
- team capability

### Operational phrasing to preserve
- better targeted mitigation
- more defensible shutdown decisions
- scalable monitoring where manual observation is hard

## Visual logic across the whole deck
### The visual system should communicate:
- overlap at the start
- system coherence early
- structured reasoning in the middle
- evidence and trust near the end
- workflow and deployment as the payoff

### Repeating motifs to use intentionally
- airspace / trajectory traces = sensing + ecology
- grid / notebook marks = research rigor
- turbine forms = operational context
- constraint highlight boxes = major insight or risk
- system nodes = deployment logic

### Color logic
- orange = urgency / energy / migration pressure / action
- teal = trust / validation / control / ecology-support
- warm paper neutral = fair-tech / civic / real-world seriousness
- dark ink = technical clarity

### Typography logic
- display font = conviction / main argument
- body sans = readability / broad-room clarity
- mono annotations = research notebook / technical credibility

---

## Main storytelling principle
This presentation should feel like:
> a guided reduction of complexity

not:
> a dump of everything we did

The audience should leave with:
- one real problem
- one strong team identity
- one clear system image
- one important insight
- one believable deployment path
