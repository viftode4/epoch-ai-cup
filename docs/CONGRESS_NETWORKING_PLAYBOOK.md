# AI Cup 2026 — Congress Presentation & Interest Playbook

This playbook is for winning **attention in the room**, not only networking after the fact.

The key idea is:

> The presentation should make the jury respect us and make the wider congress want to remember us.

That means the presentation has to be built for:
- the jury
- the wider audience in the breakout room
- the broader congress ecosystem

It should make people interested in:
- the team
- the workflow
- the system
- the deployment potential

---

## 1) Core positioning
Our strongest positioning at this congress is:

> **We are not just a model team. We are a research team with disciplined experimentation, honest validation, and a deployable system story.**

This matters because the congress theme is **Impact with AI, the Fair Tech way**.
That room will care about:
- usefulness
- responsibility
- trust
- deployment
- human oversight
- real value

So the presentation must not feel like:
- a Kaggle recap
- a benchmark diary
- a stack of model names

It should feel like:
- a serious team solving a hard real-world problem well

---

## 2) What the room should remember
By the end of the talk, people should remember five things:

### 1. The problem
Wind energy and biodiversity share the same airspace.

### 2. The team
We worked with unusual discipline and intensity.

### 3. The workflow
We used a serious research loop:
- hypothesis
- experiment
- validation
- keep/reject

### 4. The insight
The real challenge was not just classification, but **robustness under seasonal shift**.

### 5. The outcome
We built a system that is not only competitive, but also more realistic to deploy.

---

## 3) What makes the presentation “juicy” for everyone
The room is mixed. So the pitch has to contain value for multiple audience types at once.

### For jurors
- rigor
- deployability
- ecology
- trustworthiness

### For researchers
- method
- validation
- honest rejection of weak ideas

### For startups / investors / ecosystem people
- strong team
- execution speed
- differentiated workflow
- product potential

### For policy / public-sector / EU-minded people
- responsible AI
- human oversight
- public value
- deployable governance-minded system

### For industry / energy / operations people
- practical mitigation support
- reliability under changing conditions
- operational relevance

---

## 4) Best presentation ratio
To maximize congress-wide interest, use roughly this balance:

- **30%** problem + why it matters
- **25%** workflow + experimentation discipline
- **20%** system architecture
- **15%** results + evidence
- **10%** deployment + impact + next step

This is better for the congress than a model-heavy pitch.

---

## 5) The strongest congress angle
The strongest congress-wide story is:

> We turned a hard ecological sensing problem into a rigorous, deployable AI system by using a disciplined research workflow.

That works because it sells:
- societal importance
- technical capability
- maturity
- future potential

---

## 6) Slides should do this work
### Slide 1 — Why this matters
Hit the problem hard and clearly.

### Slide 2 — Our approach as a team
Show the workflow, not just the solution.

### Slide 3 — The system
Simple architecture visual.

### Slide 4 — What we learned
Seasonal shift, honest validation, what changed our design.

### Slide 5 — Results
Only the most important evidence.

### Slide 6 — Deployment / real-world use
How this supports monitoring, mitigation, human decisions.

### Slide 7 — Final message
Why the team and system matter beyond the contest.

---

## 7) What to say explicitly
The following ideas should be said out loud, not only implied:

- We did **not** just optimize a score.
- We built a **research workflow** that let us reject weak ideas.
- Our team learned that **generalization under shift** mattered more than local gains.
- We used that to build a more **credible system**.
- We even won the award for **most submissions**, which reflected disciplined experimentation.

### Good phrasing
- “We built for generalization, not just local leaderboard gains.”
- “We are not just presenting a model; we are presenting a way of building deployable AI for a hard problem.”
- “Our strongest contribution is the combination of system design, validation discipline, and deployment thinking.”

---

## 8) Venue vibe and how to win it
### Likely vibe
- energetic
- visual
- fast-moving
- social
- cross-sector
- not patient with clutter

### How to win it
- open strong in the first 20 seconds
- use clean visuals
- keep one memorable insight
- have one memorable team signal
- end with a line people can repeat afterwards

### The room-friendly version of the project
> We built a responsible AI system that turns difficult radar data into practical support for biodiversity-aware wind-energy decisions.

---

## 9) What will make people interested in us afterwards
People are more likely to care after the talk if they think:
- these people are serious
- these people can build
- these people can explain
- these people understand the real-world stakes
- these people might be useful collaborators / hires / partners

So the presentation must not only be impressive.
It must make the team feel:
- credible
- sharp
- thoughtful
- worth approaching

---

## 10) What to avoid
- too many model names
- too much benchmark language
- dense tables on slides
- overclaiming certainty
- acting like deployment is easy
- making the workflow invisible
- hiding failures and trade-offs

---

## 11) The most important lines to keep
### Team identity
> We are not just a model team; we are a research team with disciplined experimentation, honest validation, and a deployable system story.

### Problem identity
> Wind energy and biodiversity share the same airspace.

### Key insight
> The real challenge was not just classification, but robustness under seasonal shift.

### Closing identity
> The strength of our work is not only that it performs well, but that it is rigorous, ecologically grounded, and realistically deployable.
