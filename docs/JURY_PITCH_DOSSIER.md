# Jury Pitch Dossier — AI Cup 2026 Congress

_Last updated: 2026-04-11_

## Purpose
This dossier is for **pitch preparation**, not personal manipulation. It is grounded in **public professional information** and inferences from each juror’s role, employer, public writing, and subject-matter focus. It does **not** attempt to infer private vulnerabilities, private beliefs, or non-public emotional traits.

Use it to:
- adapt emphasis without changing the truth
- anticipate what each juror is likely to reward or challenge
- tune examples, framing, and closing language
- prepare Q&A answers that resonate with each person’s professional lens

---

## Universal positioning for this jury
Across the whole panel, the strongest meta-story is:

> We did not brute-force a leaderboard. We built a research-driven, evidence-based, deployment-aware system for radar bird classification under ecological and operational constraints.

Core pitch themes that work across all five jurors:
1. **Real problem** — better bird-aware monitoring for nature-inclusive wind energy.
2. **Rigorous workflow** — hypothesis -> experiment -> honest validation -> keep/reject.
3. **Technical depth with restraint** — ranking-aware modeling, shift-aware validation, domain-grounded features.
4. **Deployment realism** — decision support, not magic automation.
5. **Responsible use** — uncertainty, trade-offs, limitations, human oversight.
6. **Team execution** — disciplined experimentation, including winning the contest award for most submissions.

- no discussion of seasonality or migration context
- acting like a 0.59/0.63 score directly equals field performance

## Best framing moves

Frame the project as **a technical contribution to nature-inclusive wind-energy operations**.

### Slide hooks

- pragmatic
- field-aware
- impact-conscious
- careful with overclaiming

- "How would this system be deployed around wind farms in practice?"
- "What happens when the model is uncertain?"
- "How would you handle the cost of false positives versus false negatives?"
- "Why should we expect this to remain useful under changing seasonal conditions?"

## Best answer style for him
Use a **risk-aware engineering answer**:
1. identify operational use case
2. acknowledge uncertainty
3. explain mitigation logic
4. connect to deployment pipeline

Example structure:

Because migration patterns shift over time, we validated across months and selected features that were stable rather than only locally predictive.

## Tactical note
For Joep, you should explicitly say that you optimized not only for score but for **cross-month robustness**, because that is the closest competition proxy to operational stability.

Geert-Jan is likely to care deeply about:

- system coherence
- data-to-decision architecture
- reproducibility and information quality
- whether evaluation matches the real challenge
- whether the team understands where AI adds value and where it does not

## Working mental model for Q&A
He may test whether your reasoning is internally consistent:
- did your validation design actually reflect the problem?
- did your model choice align with the metric?
- can you justify each major design decision?
- what evidence led you to reject some techniques?
- how trustworthy is your claimed generalization?

## What he is likely to respond to positively
- "Once we recognized macro-mAP is a ranking metric, we adapted the training strategy accordingly."
- "We used stricter cross-month validation to avoid misleading results from weaker splits."
- "Every major component in the final architecture was tied back to experiment evidence or research."
- "We treated the project as an information system problem, not just a black-box optimization problem."

## What may weaken trust with him
- fuzzy methodology
- causal-sounding claims without evidence
- no honest discussion of leakage, overfitting, or validation mismatch
- saying something is robust without describing how that was tested

## Best framing moves
### Narrative angle
Frame the project as **a principled architecture born from evidence, not trial-and-error chaos**.

### Slide hooks
- one visual that shows data -> feature families -> model families -> ensemble -> output
- one slide called "Design choices justified by validation"
- one short mention of methods you explicitly rejected and why

### Tone to use
- rigorous
- calm
- precise
- non-hype

## Best likely questions from him
- "Why should we believe your model generalizes?"
- "How did your validation strategy change your design decisions?"
- "Which part of your system mattered most and how do you know?"
- "How reproducible is your workflow?"

## Best answer style for him
Use an **evidence chain answer**:
1. problem observation
2. methodological choice
3. validation evidence
4. resulting implication

Example structure:

So our confidence is not that the model is universally solved, but that our final system is better aligned with the real generalization challenge.

## Tactical note
He is likely to appreciate explicit acknowledgment that honest validation reduced optimistic results. That signals scientific maturity rather than weakness.

### Public work themes worth assuming he knows well
- weather radar as a scalable sensor for otherwise invisible aerial movement
- seasonal and nightly variation in migration intensity
- forecasting migration for curtailment / mitigation timing
- limitations of radar-derived biological inference
- mapping bird distributions to support planning, permitting, and conservation

## Likely professional lens
Bart is the juror most likely to ask: **Does this model make ecological sense?**

He is likely to care about:
- whether the features reflect real movement and environmental mechanisms
- whether you understand what radar can and cannot tell you biologically
- whether seasonal change and migration behavior are treated seriously
- whether your outputs are useful for conservation / mitigation rather than just classification
- whether your claims respect uncertainty in ecological systems

He may probe whether you:
- used domain knowledge or just generic tabular ML
- understand that bird movement is structured by season, conditions, and behavior
- appreciate the limits of group/species classification from radar signatures
- can articulate why trajectory geometry and environmental context matter biologically
- understand the difference between **prediction**, **forecasting**, and **operational decision support**
- can separate what radar measures directly from what you infer statistically

## What he is likely to respond to positively

- "We optimized for forecasting and decision support, not just classification accuracy."
- "We used honest validation because ecological systems shift across seasons and weather regimes."
- "Radar is a proxy sensor, so we treat outputs as probabilistic ecological evidence, not direct species truth."

- overclaiming biological certainty
- talking as if radar directly gives exact species identity with no ambiguity
- no ecological explanation for features
- no limitation slide or no acknowledgment of uncertainty
- treating the challenge as a generic ML benchmark with no migration ecology framing
- implying that a single model output should directly drive intervention without uncertainty handling

## Best framing moves
### Narrative angle
Frame the project as **computational ecology informed by ML**, not ML pasted onto ecology.

### Slide hooks
- a clean feature-family slide: movement, radar, environment, derived ecological context
- one line explaining why cross-month robustness matters biologically
- one line on responsible deployment: expert-in-the-loop and uncertainty-aware use

### Tone to use
- respectful of domain nuance
- biologically grounded
- measured
- purpose-oriented
- careful with uncertainty language
- specific about operational value

## Best likely questions from him
- "How biologically meaningful are your features?"
- "What are the main ecological limits of your system?"
- "How would this support conservation or mitigation decisions in practice?"
- "Why should trajectory features help distinguish these groups?"
- "How do you know the model generalizes across migration seasons?"
- "What do you do with uncertain or ambiguous radar nights?"
- "How does this translate into a real mitigation decision?"

## Best answer style for him
Use a **mechanism-based answer**:
1. ecological intuition
2. feature representation
3. observed effect in validation
4. limitation
5. deployment implication

Example structure:
> Different bird groups differ not just in raw radar return but also in movement profile and environmental context. That is why we combined trajectory-shape features, radar-derived signatures, and weather/context variables. It improves discrimination, but we still treat predictions as probabilistic support because ecology is variable and the sensing modality has limits.

## Tactical note
For Bart, your best move is to explicitly say the system is useful because it respects ecological structure instead of flattening everything into a generic classifier.
